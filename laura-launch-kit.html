Welcome to the Hold Space Challenge. I'm so glad you're here.

Before we dive in, I want to say something: the fact that you signed up for this already tells me something about you. You care — deeply — about the people in your life. You want to show up better for them. And you've probably spent a lot of time wondering why connection sometimes feels so hard, even with the people you love most.

Me too. That's actually how I found this work.

Over the next five days, I'm going to share the one skill that shifted everything for me and for the women I coach. It's called holding space.

Holding space isn't about having the right words. It's not about fixing anything. It's the art of being so fully present with someone that they feel safe enough to be completely themselves — without needing to perform, shrink, or be strong for you.

It sounds simple. But most of us were never taught how to do it. We were taught to fix, to advise, to silver-lining, to make people feel better as quickly as possible. And all of that — as well-intentioned as it is — actually creates distance.

So for the next five days, we're going to slow that down. One small practice a day. Nothing overwhelming. Just you, a quiet moment, and a shift in how you're with people.

Today's practice is just this: notice.

The next time someone shares something with you — a frustration, a worry, even something small — just notice your first impulse. Do you want to fix it? Minimise it? Relate it back to yourself? There's no wrong answer. Just noticing is the beginning.

Day 2
The Listening Shift — Why You Keep Getting Interrupted
Subject Line Options
  • Why your good intentions are pushing people away
  • The moment I realised I wasn't really listening
  • Day 2: The one thing that blocks real connection

Hi [First Name],

Hope you had a moment to try yesterday's practice — even for just a minute.

Today I want to talk about something that nobody likes to admit.

Most of us aren't actually listening when we think we are. We're waiting to respond.

There's a difference. Real listening — the kind that makes someone feel truly heard — requires something we don't usually give: space. Space for silence. Space for someone to find their words. Space for emotion to surface without us rushing in to smooth it over.

When someone says "I'm just so overwhelmed" and we immediately say "but look at everything you've achieved!" — we mean it kindly. But what we're actually communicating is: your feelings are a problem I need to solve.

It doesn't feel good to be on the receiving end of that. Even when it comes from love.

The shift is small but powerful: instead of solving, try staying.

That might sound like: "Tell me more." Or: "That sounds really hard." Or sometimes — and this is the brave one — just silence. A nod. Staying present without filling the gap.

Today's practice: In your next real conversation, count to three before you respond. Three seconds of space. See what comes up — for them, and for you.

It might feel uncomfortable. That discomfort? That's where the magic is.

See you tomorrow.

With warmth,
Laura

Your Body Knows — The Somatic Side of Connection
Subject Line Options
  • Day 3: What your body does in hard conversations
  • Your nervous system is speaking — are you listening?
  • This is why you shut down (or blow up) under pressure

Hi [First Name],

How did yesterday go? Did those three seconds of silence feel like three years? (They do, at first. You're doing great.)

Today we're going somewhere a little deeper. We're going into the body.

Here's something I've noticed with almost every woman I've worked with: when a hard conversation happens, we leave our bodies. We go into our heads. We start managing — managing the other person, managing our own reaction, managing how we look.

And from that place, we can't actually hold space for anyone. Because we're not even present with ourselves.

You can't give someone else what you don't have. Presence starts inside you.

When you feel tension rise in a conversation — a tight chest, a clenched jaw, the urge to either flee or fight — that's your nervous system doing its job. But it's also information. It's telling you something needs attention before you can really be with the other person.

Today's practice: Before your next meaningful conversation, spend one minute with one hand on your heart. Take three slow breaths. Let your shoulders drop. Ask yourself: am I actually here right now?

This isn't woo-woo. This is regulation. And a regulated nervous system is the foundation of every meaningful connection.

Day 4
The Hard One — Holding Space When You're Hurting Too
Subject Line Options
  • Day 4: What to do when you're the one who needs support
  • You can't pour from an empty cup (and what to do about it)
  • The most important person to hold space for is you

Hi [First Name],

We need to talk about something that often gets left out of conversations about connection.

What happens when you're the one who's struggling — but someone still needs you to be present?

This comes up a lot for the women I work with. Partners, friends, kids, parents — they all need something from you. And there's a quiet, persistent pressure to be "together," to be strong, to hold space for others even when nobody's holding space for you.

And here's the honest truth: you can't do it well from that place. Not really.

Holding space for others without holding space for yourself first isn't generosity. It's self-abandonment.

The most connected, caring women I know are also the women who've learned to ask for what they need. Who can say to a partner: "I want to be there for you, and I also need ten minutes first." Who can reach out to a friend and say: "I'm not okay. Can you just listen for a minute?"

That's not weakness. That's the full practice.

Today's practice: Ask yourself: who holds space for me? And if the answer is "nobody" or "I don't let them" — sit with that for a moment. What would it feel like to let someone in?

Tomorrow is Day 5 — our last day together. I have something special for you. Watch your inbox.
